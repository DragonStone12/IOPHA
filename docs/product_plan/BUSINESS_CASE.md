# The Business Problem

Our predictive models identify users at high risk for obesity based on lifestyle data, but early detection alone is insufficient. Without timely intervention and prevention strategies, these individuals face severe long-term health complications. Untreated obesity leads to chronic conditions like diabetes and heart disease, significantly increasing long-term healthcare costs for both the user and the provider. Currently, there is a gap between identifying the risk and providing the immediate, actionable prevention needed to alter the health trajectory. Furthermore, there is a friction point in connecting these high-risk users to immediate, in-network medical professionals. Users are often left without a seamless pathway to schedule an appointment with a nearby physician within the hospital's organization, or to receive reliable, immediate health guidance if they are hesitant to seek medical care right away.

# The Business Outcome

Deploying an interactive health assistant immediately after risk detection bridges this gap by offering a dual-pathway for intervention. The system links users to the nearest available doctor within the hospital's specific organization. For example, someone completing a survey on Baylor's website will be directly connected to a Baylor physician. This smooth connection helps attract more patients and makes better use of the facility. Crucially, it also provides immediate, evidence-based health information for users who choose not to see a physician right away. By offering both options simultaneously—connecting them to a doctor while also providing helpful, actionable advice—the system ensures no high-risk individual is left without support. This approach improves long-term health outcomes, reduces the likelihood of costly chronic disease management, maximizes user engagement across all comfort levels, and creates a direct pathway to monetize through in-network clinical visits and premium preventative wellness programs.

# How Machine Learning (RAG) Will Solve It

A Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system translates the initial risk prediction into a personalized prevention dialogue and a targeted care-routing mechanism. It prevents the language model from generating generic fitness tips or irrelevant clinic locations by grounding every response in verified clinical guidelines, the user's specific profile, and the hospital organization's real-time physician directory.

First, the text encoder converts the user's question and risk profile into a dense vector. Second, the system performs a nearest neighbor search across two distinct databases: one to retrieve exact preventative care guidelines and the user's stored lifestyle constraints, and another to retrieve the closest available physicians and facilities within the specific hospital network (determined by the website domain the user entered from). Third, the language model synthesizes this information to offer a comprehensive, dual-option response.

## Example

A user completes a health survey on Baylor's website and triggers a high-risk alert. They ask how to manage their weight. The system retrieves clinical guidelines on obesity prevention and cross-references the user's location with the Baylor physician directory. The model generates a dual-action response:

> "To help manage your risk, here are three evidence-based dietary adjustments you can start today, such as adjusting your meal timing to align with your waking hours. Additionally, because early intervention is highly effective, we recommend a professional consultation. I have located Dr. Smith at the closest Baylor facility, just 2 miles from your location. Would you like me to schedule an appointment with Dr. Smith, provide you with more daily health tips, or both?"
